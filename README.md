#  Retail Sales Driver Analysis: Regression Project

##  Business Problem Summary
The leadership team of a retail chain needs to understand the primary factors driving monthly sales performance across its network of stores. By identifying whether operational levers (such as marketing spend, inventory availability, and staffing) or locational factors (region and store type) have the strongest association with revenue, leadership can strategically allocate resources, adjust discounting strategies, and optimize store operations to maximize profitability.

##  Dataset Description
The analysis utilizes a cross-sectional dataset of 320 retail stores (`store_performance.csv`). The data includes operational metrics, geographic information, customer sentiment, and financial performance for a single reporting period. Missing values in continuous variables (like competitor distance and customer rating) were imputed using median values to preserve the integrity of the dataset for regression modeling.

##  Dependent and Independent Variables
* **Dependent Variable (Target):** `monthly_sales` - The continuous numeric metric representing the total revenue generated by a store.
* **Independent Variables (Predictors):** 
    * *Numerical:* `marketing_spend`, `footfall`, `avg_discount_pct`, `staff_count`, `inventory_availability_pct`, `competitor_distance_km`, `customer_rating`.
    * *Categorical:* `region` (East, North, South, West), `store_type` (Airport, High Street, Mall, Residential).
    * *Binary:* `holiday_flag`.
    * *(Note: `store_id` and downstream metrics like `monthly_profit` were excluded to prevent target leakage).*

##  Regression Approach
Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) linear regression was used to evaluate the associations between the independent variables and monthly sales. 
1.  **Simple Linear Regression:** Used initially to isolate the impact of singular metrics like marketing spend and customer footfall.
2.  **Multiple Linear Regression:** Used to evaluate the concurrent impact of multiple variables, controlling for confounding factors and minimizing omitted variable bias.
3.  **Residual Analysis:** Used to identify outlier stores (overachievers and underachievers) by calculating the difference between actual sales and model-predicted sales.

##  Dummy Variable Approach
Because standard regression models require purely numerical inputs, categorical variables (`region` and `store_type`) were transformed using **One-Hot Encoding** (Dummy Variables). 
To avoid perfect multicollinearity (the "Dummy Variable Trap"), one reference category was dropped from each group:
* **Region Baseline:** East
* **Store Type Baseline:** Airport
All resulting coefficients for regions and store types represent the expected difference in sales compared to an Airport store located in the East.

##  Model Comparison Summary
* **Simple Regression (Marketing Focus):** Explains only **16.7%** of the variance in sales. While it shows a positive ROI for marketing, it completely ignores dominant operational drivers.
* **Simple Regression (Footfall Focus):** Explains **73.6%** of the variance, proving that physical traffic is the primary baseline driver of revenue.
* **Multiple Regression (Comprehensive):** Explains **82.7%** of the variance by combining footfall, inventory levels, marketing, and categorical locational data. 

##  Final Model Selected
The **Multiple Linear Regression Model** was selected as the final framework. It provides the highest explanatory power (82.7%) and contextualizes variables accurately (e.g., showing that marketing's impact is much smaller once footfall is accounted for). It is highly actionable for strategic decision-making.

##  Business Recommendation
1.  **Focus on Traffic & Supply Chain:** Footfall ($33.86 per visitor) and Inventory Availability ($2,961 per 1% increase) are the strongest operational drivers. Prioritize localized events to drive traffic and fortify supply chains to prevent stockouts.
2.  **Audit Underperforming Formats:** "High Street" and "Residential" formats significantly underperform compared to Malls and Airports. Halt expansion of these formats until their conversion issues are resolved.
3.  **Investigate Residual Outliers:** Dispatch regional managers to audit the top 5 "Negative Residual" stores. These locations have high traffic and inventory but are failing to convert, indicating severe layout, staff, or local competitor issues.

##  Assumptions and Limitations
* **Correlation vs. Causation:** The regression model identifies strong mathematical *associations*, but it does not definitively prove causality. (e.g., Higher marketing spend is associated with higher sales, but leadership may simply be giving larger marketing budgets to stores that already perform well).
* **Linearity:** The model assumes relationships are linear and does not inherently account for diminishing returns (e.g., adding inventory eventually stops yielding linear revenue gains).
* **Omitted Variables:** The model explains ~83% of the variance; the remaining ~17% is driven by uncaptured factors like local macroeconomic conditions, specific competitor pricing, and hyper-local management quality.
